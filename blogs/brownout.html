How to Fix Brownout Issues

Why microcontrollers reset under load and the practical fixes that stop it.

PowerBrownoutDebugMCU

What is a brownout?

A brownout happens when supply voltage dips below the MCU’s safe level. The brownout detector resets the MCU to avoid corrupted execution.

How brownouts show up

  • Resets when motors/servos start or Wi‑Fi transmits
  • Relays energize → MCU reboots
  • I2C devices vanish or serial output gets corrupted
  • Boot loops on weak USB/battery power

Why they happen

  • Sudden current spikes (motors, servos, RF bursts)
  • Regulator can’t handle transients or dropout
  • Poor decoupling or long/high‑impedance wiring
  • Noisy loads sharing the MCU rail
  • Weak cables/contacts causing voltage drop

How to confirm

  • Check reset reason flags (e.g., ESP32 reset reason)
  • Scope VDD at the MCU during the event (multimeters miss fast dips)

Fixes that work

  1. Decoupling: 100 nF at every VDD pin; 1–10 µF nearby.
  2. Bulk caps for spikes: 100–470 µF near motors/relays; 10–47 µF near MCU rail.
  3. Separate rails: Motor/servo supply isolated from MCU rail; common ground plane/star point.
  4. Better regulator: Adequate current, low dropout, correct caps, good transient response.
  5. Wiring: Short, thick cables; avoid breadboards for high current; good connectors.
  6. Firmware staging: Ramp PWM, don’t power everything at once, delay RF transmit until stable.
  7. BOD configuration: Keep brownout detection on; adjust threshold only if power is proven solid.

Quick checklist

  • Do motors/relays/Wi‑Fi trigger resets?
  • Is the regulator sized for peak and transient load?
  • 100 nF at each VDD, bulk caps near load and MCU?
  • Are noisy loads on a separate rail from the MCU?
  • Is wiring/cable resistance causing a dip?
  • Can you see the VDD dip on a scope?
